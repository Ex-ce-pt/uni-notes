DNA → RNA

For some genes, the RNA is the final product

**Coding RNA** - RNA that is encoding the genetic information (mRNA).
**Non-coding RNA** - RNA that fulfils other functions.

**rRNA** - ribosomal RNA; helps with protein synthesis.
**tRNA** - transfer RNA; carries amino acids for protein synthesis.
**mRNA** - messenger RNA; carries the genetic information from the DNA into the ribosome.
**siRNA** - silencing RNA; inhibits the production of a specific protein, used in therapy.

Ribosome is mostly RNA.

## RNA structure

RNA forms complex secondary structures.
Pretty much any base can form $\ce{H}$ bond to any other base (including same pairs).
These bonds are different to the conventional Watson-Crick bonding.

**Structural motif** - specific conformation of the RNA backbone.

**Stem loop** - a very important structural motif at various stages of RNA synthesis.

## Synthesis

Not all DNA encodes genes, there is intergenetic DNA.

**Intron** - intergenetic region; regulated gene expression.
**Exon** - region that encodes a protein.

**Junk DNA** - completely nonfunctional.

#### Initiation

Starts with a promoter region.
Has lots of $\ce{\text{A}=\text{T}}$ pairs - they are weaker.

RNA Polymerase (uses a $\sigma$ factor) attaches to 35 & -10 boxes.

> The difference of 25 nucleotides between the -35 and -10 boxes means that the major groove of DNA ends up on the same side.
> RNA Polymerase can bind!

Eukaryotes have multiple different RNA Polymerase enzymes.

**Upstream promoter elements** - promoter elements further down the chain; without them transcription is inefficient.
**Null gene** - no promoter; no transcription.
**Alternative promoters** - promoters at different places; can get RNA of different size.

**TBP** - TATA box binding protein; recognizes the TATA sequence in eukaryotes.
Eukaryotic cells need at least 12 more proteins for transcription.
Needs a **mediator complex** as well.

#### Elongation

3'→5' along DNA, 5'→3' along RNA.
Each addition releases $\ce{PPi}$.

RNA Polymerase adds complementary nucleotides.
RNA Polymerase can correct mistakes.

The DNA helix is separated by the push and pull motion created by the $(+)$ and $(-)$ charged amino acids in the polymerase.

**Elongation factors** - proteins that stabilize the RNA.
**Capping** - closing the strand of RNA with a neutral group for stability.

> Bacteria modify the polymerase a little when developing resistance to antibiotics blocking RNA synthesis.

#### Termination

The last nucleotides to be added are A and U (easier to detach, again).

**Intrinsic terminators** - sequences of DNA that make RNA Polymerase make a loop and end transcription.
**Rho dependent termination** - Rho protein (helicase) scans the RNA for a terminator loop and detaches the polymerase.

CPSF & CstF - factors in eukaryotes that perform polyadenylation (adding a lot of As).

## RNA processing

**Cutting/End trimming**

E.g. the rRNA genes are encoded together, so when it's transcribed, it needs to be cut into functional pieces.
The cutting is usually done next to a stem loop.

---

**Splicing**

In eukaryotes, introns are removed from the RNA.
On average in humans there are 9 introns in every transcribed RNA.
**snRNP** - ribonucleoproteins.
snRNPs attach to specific nucleotides in the RNA, attract, then the bulge formed is cleaved off.
**Spliceosome** - a complex of snRNPs that performs the splicing.

---

**Self-splicing**

RNA can splice itself.
**Ribozymes** - RNA molecules that can act as enzymes.

---

**Alternative splicing**

Exons of the RNA are mixed and matched to make different proteins.

---

**Modifications**

**snoRNA** - modifies the RNA in one particular way.
