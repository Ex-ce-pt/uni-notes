

Functions:
- energy source
- cell membranes
- structure
- signal transmission
- immunology


Commonly bound to proteins - glycoproteins


Types:
- Monosaccarides - single units
- Oligosaccarides - several units
- Polysaccarides



consist of: carbonyl + several OH groups

simplest:
D-glyceraldehyde
dihydroxyacetone

Almost all monosaccarides have OH groups attached to asymmetric centers.

The configuration of the sugar is the configuration of the chiral center that is the farthest from the carbonyl group

the linear structure of monosaccarides is written in a Fischer projection
circular - Haworth projection

nomenclature:
aldehyde - aldose
ketone - ketose

\[al/ket]\[# of C]-ose

e.g. - glycose is an aldehexose


all monosaccarides w/ 5+ carbons form cycles
form hemiacetals


often monosaccarides are modified with other functional groups






Oligosaccarides

linked together monosaccarides
bby condensation reaction between one hemiacetal and one alcohol OH group
O-glycosidic bond

nomenclature: alpha-1,4,glycosidic bond

> The monosaccaride donating the hemiacetal OH cannot become linear any more!
> The last monosaccaride in the chain can become linear

> Storing light metabolites in polymers means that the osmotic pressure stays low.

Glycogen - energy storage in animal cells. Liver/muscle
Starch - energy storage in plants. Amylose/amylopectin
Cellulose - structure in plants. Most abundant organic molecule on the planet!
Chitin - exoskeleton of some animals

Amylose - unbranched glucose by alpha-1-4 bonds
Amylopectin - occasionally branched with alpha-1-6 bonds

glycogen is like amylopectin, but more branched
forms clumps visible in a microscope


cellulose is unbranched glucose with beta-1-4 bonds
dense fibers with high resistance
network of hydrogen bonds between neighbouring chains


chitin is second most abundant in nature
unbranched N-acetylglucosamine with beta-1-4 bonds


glycoproteins

carbohydrates covalently attached to proteins
sugar part points outside
often component of cell membranes

Glycan - sugar chain attached to a protein

N-linked - connected by the nitrogen of the AA side chain (e.g. Asp)
O-linked - connected by the oxygen of the AA side chain (e.g. Ser/Thr)
