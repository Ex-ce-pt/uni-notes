## Fatty acids

Carboxylic acids; $\ce{C4}$ to $\ce{C36}$, commonly between $\ce{C12}$ and $\ce{C24}$.

In monounsaturated, double bond usually at $\ce{C}9$ or $\ce{C}10$.
In polyunsaturated, usually at $\ce{C}12$ and $\ce{C}15$ (1 methylene $\ce{-CH2-}$ group in between).

> Fatty acids are almost always cis.

#### Shorthand nomenclature

Normally historical names are used.

\[# of C]:\[# of double bonds] $\Delta$ \[position of double bonds]

Examples:
$18:1(\Delta^9)$, cis-9-octadecenoic acid
```smiles
CCCCCCCC/C=C\CCCCCCCC(=O)O
```

$18:2(\Delta^{9,12})$, cis-cis-9,12-octadecadieneoic acid
```smiles
CCCCC/C=C\C/C=C\CCCCCCCC(=O)O
```


$20:3(\Delta^{5,8,11})$, cis-cis-cis-5,8,11-eicosatrieneoic acid
```smiles
CCCCCCCC/C=C\C/C=C\C/C=C\CCCC(O)O
```

#### Alternative nomenclature (Omega)

Tells the position of the 1st double bond counting from the last carbon.

omega-3/$\omega$-3 - first double bond at 3 from the end.

#### Physical properties

Insoluble in water.
Carboxylic group is polar and at physiological pH deprotonated.

At room temperature:
	Saturated acids are waxy - chains can pack closely forming something reminiscent of a solid.
	Unsaturated acids - oily liquids.

## Triacylglycerols

Simplest lipids.
Esters of 3 fatty acids and glycerol.
Nonpolar & hydrophobic.

Functions:
- insulation
- lipid droplets in adipocytes
- In plant seeds for nutritional reserve

Glycerol:
```smiles
C(O)C(O)C(O)
```

## Cellular membranes

Lipids are **amphipathic** - they have both hydrophobic and hydrophilic ends.

In water, lipids cluster together immediately - hydrophilic parts point outside, hydrophobic parts point inside.
If the lipids are small & wedge-shaped → a **micelle** forms - a small ball with the hydrophobic residues inside.
If the lipids are large & cylindrical → a **bilayer** forms - membrane separating 2 aqueous environments.
If the lipids are asymmetrical → a **vesicle** forms - ball, water inside, water outside, practically a cell.

Most biological lipids are curved, so they form membranes (vesicles), rather than micelles.

Different membranes have unique compositions & unique proteins interacting with them.

**Peripheral membrane proteins** - loosely associated with one side of the membrane.
**Integral proteins** - inside the membrane.

Proteins can go through the membrane one or multiple times
~20 AAs to cross a membrane.
Hydrophobic AAs inside the membrane, hydrophilic outside.
Usual structure of the segment inside the membrane: alpha helix or beta barrel.

**GPI anchors** - attach proteins to the surface of the membranes.

#### Membrane dynamics 

**Fluid mosaic model** - membrane consists of various components that can feely move within the surface.

Lipids move very fast inside the membrane laterally, but very slowly from one side to another ("flip-flop" movement).

Enzymes catalysis:
- Flippase - aminophospholipid from outside + ATP → lipid inside
- Floppase - sterols & other lipids inside + ATP → lipids outside
- Scramblase - maintains equilibrium between lipids inside & outside using $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$

The speed of lipid movement can be measured using **FRAP** - fluorescence recovery after photobleaching.
Bleach a dot on a cell, see how fast it diffuses.

## Transport

Charged and large molecules cannot get through the membrane generally.
Hydrophobic molecules readily dissolve in the membrane.

**Electrochemical gradient** - separation of concentrations and charges inside and outside of the cell.
Naturally compounds want to diffuse, need energy to keep the gradient.

> 2000 genes encode proteins involved in transport across membranes.

**Passive transporters** - transporters that facilitate the exchange of the substrate according to the electrochemical gradient.
**Ion channels** - aqueous path w/ a **single** gate.
**Facilitated diffusion** - transporters dehydrate solutes, transport, rehydrate on the other side; **2 gates** on both sides.

---

**Active transporters** - transporters that transport the substrate against the electrochemical gradient.
**Primary active transporters** - use chemical energy to do work directly (ATP).
**Secondary active transporters** - transport the unfavourable substrate at a cost of transferring the favourable one.

**Symporter** - 2 substrates in the same direction.
**Antiporter** - one substrate in one direction, another substrate in another direction.

**P-type ATPases** - primary active transporters of cation that use ATP.

e.g. $\ce{Na+ K+}$ **ATPase** - 3 $\ce{Na+}$ out, 2 $\ce{K+}$ in.
The net charge of the cell becomes slightly negative!

> $\ce{[K]}_{\text{inside}} >> \ce{[K]}_{\text{outside}}$
> $\ce{[Na]}_{\text{inside}} << \ce{[Na]}_{\text{inside}}$

$\ce{Na+}$**-glucose symporter** - secondary active transporter that moves glucose inside by moving $\ce{Na+}$ inside as well.
The energy for the transfer of the glucose comes from the gradient between the inside and the outside of the cerll when it comes to the concentration of $\ce{Na+}$.
