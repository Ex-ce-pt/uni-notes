## Lipids

Insoluble in water.
Energy-rich.
Stored in triacylglycerols.
Can have other functions (messengers etc).

Fatty acid - carboxylic acids; $\ce{C4}$ to $\ce{C36}$
Commonly between 12 and 24

in monounsaturated, double bond usually at 9 or 10
in polyunsaturated usually 12 and 15
1 methylene group in between

> Fatty acids are almost always cis.

#### Glycerophospholipids

like triacylglycerols but one of the fatty acids is substituted with a phosphor group and an alcohol

can categorize depending on the group at the phosphodiester

naming - phosphatidyl + group of phosphodiester

#### Sphingolipids

Sphingosine + 1 fatty acid via amide bond + head-group

categorization depending on the head-group

sphingomyelins - subclass that assists electrical conductivity between neurons

> Niemann Pick disease type A and B

#### Sterols

signaling molecules

amphipathic - has both hydrophobic and hydrophilic ends

lipids cluster together in water - micelles

most biological lipids are curved, so they form membranes (vesicles), rather than micelles.

water inside, water outside, the membrane in the middle

membranes are lipid bilayers

fluid mosaic model - mosaic of components, everything can move in the surface of the membrane

membranes have unique compositions
unique proteins interacting with the membranes

peripheral membrane proteins - loosely associated with one side of the membrane
integral proteins - inside the membrane
peripheral protein covalently linked to lipid

~20 AAs to cross a membrane
hydrophobic AAs inside the membrane, hydrophilic outside

alpha helix or beta barrel

GPI anchors - attach proteins to the surface of the membranes





lipids move very fast inside the membrane laterally, but very slowly from one side to another

## Transport

Charged and large molecules cannot get through the membrane generally.
Hydrophobic molecules readily dissolve in the membrane.

Electrochemical gradient
separation of concentrations and charges inside and outside of the cell
naturally compounds want to diffuse
need energy to keep the gradient


2000 genes encode proteins involved in transport across membranes
many types of transporters

don't use energy, uses the gradient:
ion channels - aqueous path w/ a gate
facilitated diffusion - transporters dehydrate solutes, transport, rehydrate on the other side


primary active transport, overcomes spontaneous mixing:
uses ATP
P-type ATPases - 

e.g. Na+ K+ ATPase - 3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in
makes the cell more negatively charged

> a lot of K inside the cell, a little outside
> a little Na  inside the cell, a lot outside

antiport - one thing in one direction, another thing in another direction


secondary active transport:
uses energy of the gradient to move something else
Na+-glucode symporter - moves glucose inside by moving Na+ inside

Symport - 2 things in the same direction
