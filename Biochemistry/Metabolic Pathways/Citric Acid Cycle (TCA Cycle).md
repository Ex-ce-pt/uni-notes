Pyruvate Dehydrogenase (PDH) Complex

turns pyruvate into Acetyl-CoA to prepare it for the Citric Acid Cycle

Acetyl-CoA - CoA coenzyme + acetyl group as a thioester
high energy of hydrolysis
high transfer potential of acetyl group

> Coenzyme A has adenine and ribose in its structure.
> Cells like to reuse compounds as much as possible!

series of reactions close together to improve efficiency
**Substrate channeling** - passage of intermediates from one enzyme directly to another without release.

in the complex:
1. E1 - Pyruvate dehydrogenase - decarboxylation of pyruvate
2. E2 - Dihydrolipoyl Transacetylase - oxidation to a cation
3. E3 - Dihydrolipoyl Dehydrogenase - regeneration

uses many cofactors

1.
TPP - catalyses decarboxylation almost always
thiazolium ring can easily lose a proton and act as a nucleophile


2.
lipoic acid
has a disulfide bond
usually attached to Lysine of E2 via amide bond
can receive e^- by breaking the disulfide bond

3.
dihydrolipoamide gives the 2 hydrogens back to FAD, forming FADH2
then, FADH2 transfers them to NAD, forming NADH
FAD is there, because it]s covalently bonded to E3, making sure that the hydrogens get transferred quickly and efficiently, then NAD takes them and escapes

TCA CYCLE!!!

cyclic pathway

Acetyl-CoA donates the acetyl group to the cycle
every iteration - 1 acetyl enters, 2 CO2 leave
oxidation/decarboxylation reactions or preparation for those
products: e^- in NADH and FADH2

the cycle has some use in anabolism




generation of citrate is very exergonic - drives the reaction forward





decarboxylations are very energetically favourable
CO2 is very inert and stable → generating it yields a lot of energy


products of the cycle:
1 ATP (via GTP)
3 NADH
1 FADH2
2 CO2


amphibolic pathway - involved in both catabolism and anabolism

intermediates can be withdrawn to synthesise other molecules:
- nucleotides
- amino acids
- glucose
