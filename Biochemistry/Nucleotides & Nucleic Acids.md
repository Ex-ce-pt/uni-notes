
## Nucleobases

Purines:
- Adenine (A)
- Guanine (G)

Pyrimidines:
- Cytosine (C)
- Thymine (T)
- Uracil (U)

![[_media/Information Pathways/Nucleotides & Nucleic Acids/nucleobases.png]]

Complementarity:
	$\ce{\text{C}#\text{G}}$ - 2 $\ce{H}$ bonds
	$\ce{\text{A}=\text{T}}$, $\ce{\text{A}=\text{U}}$ - 3 $\ce{H}$ bonds

Since C and G are linked by 3 $\ce{H}$ bonds, these are harder to separate than other nucleotides.

In one complementary pair there is a purine and a pyrimidine nucleobase.

## Nucleotides

**Nucleotides** - phosphate + ribose + nucleobase
**Nucleoside** - ribose + nucleobase

![[_media/Information Pathways/Nucleotides & Nucleic Acids/nucleotide-nucleoside.png]]

> n' means the nth carbon of the ribose sugar.
> The carbons of the nucleobase are numbered without '

Sugars used:
	**RNA → $\beta$-Ribose**
	**DNA → $\beta$-2'-deoxyribose**

The nucleobases are linked to ribose sugars with $\beta$-glycosidic linkage.

| Deoxynucleotide (DNA) | Nucleoside     | Letter | Ribonucleotide (RNA) | Nucleoside | Letter |
| --------------------- | -------------- | ------ | -------------------- | ---------- | ------ |
| Deoxycytidylate       | Deoxycytidine  | C/dC   | Cytidylate           | Cytidine   | C      |
| Deoxyguanylate        | Deoxyguanosine | G/dG   | Guanylate            | Guanosine  | G      |
| Deoxyadenylate        | Deoxyadenosine | A/dA   | Adenylate            | Adenosine  | A      |
| Deoxythymidylate      | Deoxythymidine | T/dT   | Uridylate            | Uridine    | U      |

Nucleobases can be modified to slightly alter their functions.
- Methylation
- Hydroxymethylation
- etc...

## Nucleic acids

Phosphodiester linkage between 5'-phosphate group of one nucleotide and 3'-$\ce{OH}$ group of the next.

![[_media/Information Pathways/Nucleotides & Nucleic Acids/nucleic-acids.png]]

> DNA molecules are polymerized going 5'→3'.
> 5' is the last $\ce{PO4^{3-}}$ group.
> 3' is the $\ce{OH}$ group.

**Primary structure** - linear structure of nucleotides linked covalently.
**Secondary structure** - stable structure with hydrogen bonds and van der Waals interactions between the bases.
**Tertiary structure** - complex folding of large chromosomes, tRNA or rRNA.

DNA is an **antiparallel double helix**.
**Antiparallel double helix** - one strand going one way, one going another.

> The lack of the $\ce{OH}$ group in the nucleotides of DNA makes it possible to form a double helix.
> If $\ce{OH}$ were there, there would be steric hindrance (like in RNA).

In the helix, there is a **major groove** and a **minor groove** due to the angle between the ribose and phosphate.
Major groove is more accessible, so proteins normally read information through it.

![[_media/Information Pathways/Nucleotides & Nucleic Acids/grooves.png]]

Metal cations stabilize the negative charge of the phosphate groups - $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$.

Glycosidic bond:
Can be either Syn (nucleobase is directly above the ribose sugar) or Anti (nucleobase is pointing away from the ribose sugar).

Ribose isomery:
Ribose can either be C2'-endo or C3'-endo, depending on which $\ce{C}$ atom points out of the ring's plane.

#### Packing of nucleic acids

DNA is tightly packed into chromosomes a few $\mu \text{m}$ in length.
**Histones** - small basic proteins the DNA is wrapped around.

**Don't know** why different species have a different \# of chromosomes.

DNA-RNA hybrid helices are possible.

rRNA has a complex structure, but it's generally well-conserved.

## Nucleic acid damage

**Denaturation** - the helix breaking apart into 2 strands from temperature or extreme pH change.
**Annealing** - 2 strands spontaneously merging into a helix.

DNA can also be partially denatured.
$\ce{\text{A}=\text{T}}$ segments are more likely to be denatured because they have less $\ce{H}$ bonds than $\ce{\text{C}#\text{G}}$ segments.

**Mutations** - permanent changes in the DNA, usually due to aging and carcinogenesis.

**Deamination** - spontaneous loss of exocyclic $\ce{-NH2}$ groups. C → U
Deaminated C is recognised as foreign DNA and removed.
Probably why DNA doesn't have any U in the first place.
Deamination happens ~100 times/day.
$\ce{HNO2}$ and its precursors favour deamination.

**Depurination** - spontaneous hydrolysis of the bond between the ribose sugar and nucleobase.

UV light creates pyrimidine dimers.

DNAses normally lyse the exposed single-stranded DNA quickly - must protect the DNA at all times.

## Other functions

Nucleotides are used for energy storage and usage in cells.

| Nucleotide | Full name              | Function                     |
| ---------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| ATP        | Adenosine triphosphate | General energy carrier       |
| GTP        | Guanosine triphosphate | Signaling, protein synthesis |
| UTP        | Uridine triphosphate   | Activation of carbohydrates  |
| CTP        | Cytidine triphosphate  | Synthesis of phospholipids   |

Also, nucleosides are used as various signaling molecules.
